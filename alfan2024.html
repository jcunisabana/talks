Is the sense of ownership a quality of bodily sensation over and above its spatial properties?

NO

A good philosophical account of the special character of our awareness of our body "from the inside" and the sense of ownership can be found by attending to our awareness of sensations within the bounds of our felt bodies.

It gives us a powerful and simple account that is compatible with the empirical evidence available.

Let me explain.

The relational view of bodily ownership

The phenomenal properties of experience can be initially characterized as the properties that account for what it is for a person from their own point of view to have such an experience.

That distinctive feel or what it is like to be the subject of a perceptual experience is often called by philosophers the qualitative aspect of experience or its phenomenal character.

Qualia, as they are sometimes called.

However, several philosophers hold views according to which it is really difficult or even impossible to give a further characterization of what constitutes it.

Ned Block’s (1993) classic remark: ‘You ask: What is it that philosophers have called qualitative states? I answer, only half in jest: As Louis Armstrong said when asked what jazz is, “If you got to ask, you ain’t never gonna get to know.”’

Frank Jackson: Tell me everything physical there is to tell about what is going on in a living brain, the kind of states, their functional role, their relation to what goes on at other times and in other brains, and so on and so forth, and be I as clever as can be in fitting it all together, you won’t have told me about the hurtfulness of pains, the itchiness of itches, pangs of jealousy, or about the characteristic experience of tasting a lemon, smelling a rose, hearing a loud noise or seeing the sky. (Jackson 1982, p. 127)

And yet there there are some that insist in giving some kind of account of the phenomenal character of perceptual experience.

Nonetheless, there is a persistent and prevalent tendency among philosophers to assume that the distinctively first-personal knowledge we have, or can have, of our sensory experiences when we have them has a special and significant role to play when it comes to offering a philosophical account of conscious sensory experience, and that an appeal to introspection and phenomenology is legitimate when theorizing about the nature of such experience in a way it perhaps isn’t when theorizing about thought. (Soteriou 2013, p. 9)

Relational views of experience claim that the phenomenal character of an experience —what it is like to have that experience—is fundamentally about the relationship between the subject and the objects of experience. These views suggest that introspection reveals this relational nature, where the phenomenal character is not just an internal mental state but is constituted by the direct relation with the world around us.

This means that the characteristic subjective experience of, for instance, tasting a lemon is to be understood in terms of a perceptual relation one is in with a lemon and its properties.

Because of this the discussion of relational views of experience usually circles around issues about the possibility of hallucination.
The debate seems to be about on the apparently perplexing conclusion that hallucinations and a veridical perceptions as of, say, a banana do not have the same phenomenal properties despite the fact that one cannot introspectively tell them apart.

If in a hallucination there seems to be a relation but there is none, how could it be that the perceptual relation to objects and their properties constitutes the phenomenal properties of experience?

If veridical and hallucinatory experiences cannot be introspectively distinguished and only in the former there is a perceptual relation, how could it be that such relation constitutes the phenomenal character of an experience? What would the character of hallucinations be?

To characterize the phenomenal character of an experience in terms of a perceptual relation to objects and properties has the seemingly baffling consequence that introspection, even though we take it to be a source of evidence, is a fallible source of evidence because it fails to reveal the character of hallucinations.

The debate then is about the aptness of accepting the constitutive claim about the phenomenal character while at the same time taking introspection to have such an important epistemic role.

In this talk I explore a different kind of challenge to relationalism about perceptual experience.
The challenge arises not from cases where there might not be an object (like in hallucinations) but from phenomenal properties that do not seem to be accountable in terms of a relation to objects and properties.

This shifts discussion of relational views of perception away from objects and properties and turns towards structural aspects of perceptual experience.

The challenge arises from a question about the nature of bodily perception and the apparently special way in which one experiences one’s own body “from the inside”.

Let me explain.

Ordinary cases of bodily awareness like that of feeling pain in my jaw involve what seems to be a sense of ownership. In feeling pain not only do I have a sensation located in a bodily part, it seems to me like it is in a part of MY body.

(This is the kind of awareness that supports IEM judgments of one's own posture. (CF Evans 1982.))

Bodily sensations may be felt as being in a body part one does not actually have (like, for example, in a phantom limb or a rubber hand) but they are felt as if they were at bodily part of one's own.

Pain in a phantom hand is perceived as a pain in what seems to be one’s hand, even if there is no such hand. Tickles in a rubber hand are perceived in what seems to one to be one's hand, even if it is a rubber hand.

(Photo from Golan Levin)

How could a relational view of experience differentiate between the, say, tactile experiences of our own body and the kind of perceptual experience that we have of our own body “from the inside” that includes this apparent sense of ownership?

How could the phenomenal character of ownership be characterized in terms of the objects and properties that are being perceived? What experienced property would amount to an experience of ownership of the body? Ought the relationalist committ to the view that our body have a special metaphysical quality that accounts for ownership?

Ownership

Following Martin (1992, 1993, 1995), the relationalist might say that in so far there is bodily awareness at distinct spatial coordinates there is already a sense of one's own body. For a sensation to be felt at a particular location already is to be felt as if it were one's own body.

"What marks out a felt limb as one's own is not some special quality that it has, but simply that one feels it in this way." (Martin 1992: 201)

Relational view of bodily ownership

The body in which one feels sensations feels as one’s own body

One may argue against the relational view of bodily ownership by saying that there is no such awareness of ownership:

There might be awareness of one's body on the basis of which one might judge that it is our own body, but there is no proper sense of ownership

I will not discuss this form of eliminativism about the sense of ownership.

The considerations against the relational view of bodily ownership I wish to examine are those presented by Frederique de Vignemont in her book Mind the Body (2018).
Two kinds of cases used by De vignemont against the relational view of bodily ownership:
  1. Referred sensations: cases of tool-use where it is possible for a subject to non-metaphorically feel sensations in tools without having a sense of ownership of the tool as a part of their body.
  2. Somatoparaphrenia: Cases of patients suffering from a pathology may experience bodily sensations at a bodily part without experiencing that part of their body as their own.

Referred sensations

"So, further, does the woodcutter feel, along with the axe’s reaction against his hand, its hissing cleaving of the wood; so does the soldier feel his weapon piercing the flesh of his antagonist." (Lotze, 1888, pp. 588–9, quoted by De Vignemont 36)

The incorporation of tools into some body representations (Martel et al., 2016) shows that there can be some cases in which a tool might extend ones sense of one's body and yet not come with the conscious awareness of the tool as own's one like, for example, there is such awareness in the case of the rubber hand illusion.

The soldier does not consciously sense their weapon as their own body in the same conscious way in which they might feel a rubber hand in the right experimental setup.

In tool use, De Vignemont claims, a sensation can be felt at a tool and not come with the conscious awareness of the tool as one's own body. Is the relational view in problems?

No, if we reflect on the ways in which our sensory consciousness might be extended.

 
It is not clear that extensions of senses work just exactly like the regular, un-extended sense. An extension can ruffle-up the distinct character of our sensation and the sense of ownership might get lost. This failure of transporting the full character happens a lot in one of the more interesting cases of extension of the senses: sensory substitution.

Subjects using a Tactile Visual Sensory Substitution or Auditory Visual Sensory Substitution device extend their visual capacities by exploiting the sense of touch or hearing to the extent that the device takes input through a camera and produces the relevant output. (Paul Bach-y-Rita and others)

Extend?!

Yes. Their perceptual reports seem to support the idea that they can see for they recognize a range of ordinary objects, point to them in space and even tell their absolute size and distance from the subject. Some even report having a 3D sense of the environment. A good amount of what ordinary vision gives us!

And yet subjects using these devices seem to lack a basic feature of visual experience: they sense no color, something that seems imposible in regular eyesight.

Sensory substituion suggests that even if tool use extends our capacity to have bodily sensations, there is no reason to believe that bodily sensations will have the same fundamental nature as sensations felt in our body, that is, no reason to expect that they will include a sense of ownership.

Is the sense of ownership a quality of bodily sensation over and above its spatial properties?

The evidence from referred sensations in tools does not decide in favour of De Vignemont's answer.

Somatoparaphrenia

Many [patients] can still feel pain and even cry out if the examiner pinches their “alien” hand (Melzack, 1990). For instance, one patient asked his doctor:
P: I still have the acute pain where the prosthesis is.
E: Which prosthesis?
P: Don’t you see? This thing here (indicating his left arm). The doctors have attached this tool to my body in order to help me to move [...] Once home could I ask my wife, from time to time, to remove this left arm and put it in the cupboard for a few hours in order to have some relief from pain? (Maravita, 2008, p. 102).
Some pain experiences of patients with somatoparaphrenia, also known as asomatognosia or alien hand syndrome, purportedly present a counterexample to the relational view of bodily ownership for they seem to constitute cases of bodily sensation at one’s body without a sense of it being one’s own, that is, cases of sensation without ownership.

They seem to feel pain as if it were in another person's body!?!?!

Subjects are aware that they feel pain at a bodily part, but they are not aware of that pain as being in their own body. Some report feeling pain in a prosthetic arm; others report feeling pain in a relative’s or the examiner’s body part. It is a pain they have in a bodily part they do not sense as if it were their own.

How can the relationalist respond?

The evidence does not show bodily sensation with complete spatial character.

Let me explain.

To see the incomplete nature of the spatial character of such experiences, we need to tease out two aspects of spatial bodily awareness.

For even though patients suffering of somatoparaphrenia have some awareness of the spatial properties of their bodily sensations they lack an awareness of other spatial properties.

In ordinary cases, there is no question about why a bodily experience is perceived within certain bounds. In these cases, there is a sense that the limits of one’s bodily experience are those of one’s felt body; limits not merely feelt to be the same as of one’s felt body but rather as constituted by it.

One senses the bounds of possible experience as they are to the extent there is a sense in which the limits are set by one's own body.

So in ordinary cases, we have a spatial sense of the location of the sensation but also of the bounds within which those sensations could be felt.
In the case of the somatoparaphrenia, subjects lack a sense of the limits of sensation: there is no sense as to why the sensation is felt at one particular place and not, for instance, three meters away.
The subjects might feel pain in a location determined by the region in space occupied by their body, but they do not sense it as so.
It is a case where the spatial structure of their bodily sensations are bound up by the sensed body but it is not presented as so, making space for confabulation about its ownership (“it is a prosthetic”, “it is my niece’s”).

So?

One might think that cases of pains in an alien hand are not genuine bodily sensations (like cases of referred sensations).

Alternatively, I think that they are better understood as cases where subjects have a limitation in their spatial sense that explains their lack of ownership.

Ordinary bodily awareness is awareness of a sensation in a region of space that is sensed as part of a bigger space determined by one’s body.

Patients with somatoparaphrenia can have awareness of a sensation in a region of space (an arm) but this space is not sensed as being part of bigger space determined by their own body.

Their sense of their body is partial, for they have no sense of the limits of possible sensation.

Is the sense of ownership a quality of bodily sensation over and above its spatial properties?

The evidence from somatoparaphrenia does not decide in favour of De Vignemont's answer.

To conclude: The special character of bodily awareness as having a sole object, in contrast to the five traditional senses, might be bit over-emphasized.

Though bodily awareness does not involve identifying one among many possible objects of experience, more than just the body is presented.

The relationalist can account for spatial bodily awareness of two kinds. We sense the body as the place where a particular sensation is felt: the hand where I feel pain. But we also sense the body as the place where (other) sensations could be felt: the hand where I do not feel pain but where I could.
